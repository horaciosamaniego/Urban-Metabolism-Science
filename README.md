## Abstract

Understanding how scientific fields evolve is essential for anticipating their future contributions to societal challenges. Urban Metabolism (UM) has become a central framework for analysing urban sustainability, yet the structure, maturity, and internal dynamics of UM research have not been systematically assessed as properties of the  
field itself. Here, we present a science‑of‑science analysis of UM research at the intersection of cities and sustainability, examining how disciplinary composition, methodological choices, and sustainability conceptualisations shape the field's current trajectory and future potential. We analyse 792 publications indexed in the Web of   Science Core Collection using bibliometric methods, methodological classification, and qualitative synthesis. Our results reveal a rapid expansion of UM research between   2010 and 2019, followed by a stabilisation phase consistent with field maturation. Environmental science and engineering dominate the field, while urban studies and  planning remain comparatively marginal. Methodologically, UM research relies heavily on flow‑based, city‑scale analyses, with systematic gaps in scaling approaches at      national and regional levels—precisely the scales at which urban sustainability policy is most often formulated. We further show that sustainability is operationalised     through multiple, partially incompatible framings, generating conceptual fragmentation that limits cross‑study comparability and cumulative synthesis. Together, these      patterns point to methodological lock‑ins and epistemic blind spots that constrain the field’s capacity to inform long‑term urban sustainability transitions. By making     the internal structure of UM research explicit, this study provides an empirically grounded diagnosis of a maturing field at a turning point and identifies key leverage    points for integrating methods, clarifying sustainability framings, and strengthening the future relevance of urban sustainability science.
